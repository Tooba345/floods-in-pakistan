Floods in Pakistan: From Disaster to Resilience

Pakistan faces recurring and increasingly intense floods, driven by heavy monsoon rains, melting glaciers and climate change. The 2022 "mega-floods" affected tens of millions of people and became a global symbol of climate vulnerability.

Understanding Floods in Pakistan

A country caught between glaciers, rivers and monsoon extremes.

Context

Geography & major rivers

Pakistan is crossed by the Indus River and its tributaries, flowing from the Himalayas and Karakoram ranges down to the Arabian Sea. Many cities, villages and farms are located on floodplains where seasonal river swelling is common.

Climate

Monsoon & glacier melt

Most of the country's rainfall arrives during the summer monsoon. Intense rainstorms, combined with accelerated glacier melt in the north, can quickly raise river levels and trigger riverine and flash floods.

Risk

Why risk is increasing

Rapid urbanisation, settlements close to riverbeds, deforestation, poor drainage systems and climate change all increase flood frequency and severity, especially in Sindh, Balochistan and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa.

Key message: Floods in Pakistan are not only "natural disasters". Human decisions about land use, infrastructure, planning and emissions make communities much more or less vulnerable.
